01 — Model

Navigation model

The platform, onboarding, navigation depth, and the access roles that decide what each person can see and do.

Platform — mobile-first

Built for a phone in one hand, at the vet, at the kennel, on the sofa.

PetPal is mobile web first; desktop is a later adaptation. The launch market is the EU (GDPR applies to all pet & owner data; pet-passport format follows EU regulation). The bar is "easy for a 16-year-old and a 70-year-old alike" — warm and companion-like, not clinical. Because the research showed the real adoption risk is capture friction, not lack of pain, every primary action is reachable with a thumb and asks for the least possible typing.

Onboarding

Owner — the only person who signs up: create account → Set up a pet (first record) → land on the Pet dossier. With one pet, the app auto-lands on that dossier, so the main job is reachable from the very first screen. Onboarding stays deliberately thin — the dossier can be filled gradually, because forcing everything up front is exactly what makes people abandon record apps (research F4).

Receiving Caregiver — never onboards. They arrive through a shared link with no account, no install (account/auth friction is a documented drop-off driver — research F7), opening straight into the read-only Shared pet view.

Navigation depth — three levels max

Roles & access

RoleCan see / doHow they get inPersona

Sharing is the spine: roles are granted per pet via an Access grant (link/code, scoped, time-boxed) — view-only vs. limited-edit — so the same dossier shows a different, role-appropriate face to each person. This role-based, portable sharing is PetPal's documented market whitespace.

03 — Structure

Sitemap tree

Every page from sitemap.md, with the job it serves next to it. Two journeys: the owner app and the no-account recipient.

Two people, two journeys, one source of truth.

The Owner app is built for the Organised Owner primary — someone who treats their pet's health, documents and care like their own admin. Everything hangs off a single Pet dossier she can both consult and pass on. The Recipient journey is a no-account, link-only view for the Receiving Caregiver secondary (sitter / walker / new vet) who has never met the pet and needs to understand it in seconds.

Design rule: there is a job behind every screen — orphan screens were removed, orphan jobs (R4, E3) were parked rather than silently dropped. The structure is intentionally shallow so the main job is one tap from the door.

04 — Behaviour

User flows

From flows.md. 🟦 screen · 🟨 state · 🟥 error · 🟩 success · 🟧 partial (recovered). No dead ends remain.

Four flows — one main, two frequent, one high-stakes.

The first is the Organised Owner's main loop; R1 and R2 are her most frequent related jobs; R5 belongs to the secondary Worried Owner. Each diagram shows not just the happy path but the recovery from every error/empty state — because the research found the make-or-break for adoption isn't whether the pain is real (it is), it's whether the tool stays low-friction when things go wrong.

05 — Coverage

Traceability matrix

Jobs × screens. ✓ = the screen participates in the job. Highlighted rows are orphan jobs (no screen). Orphan screens Sc20/Sc21 were resolved — see note.

Why this matrix exists: nothing should be built without a job, and no job should be left homeless.

Reading it keeps the product honest. A column with no ✓ would be a screen nobody needs (we found two — Archive/delete and Account-prefs — and removed/merged them). A row with no ✓ is a job with nowhere to do it (R4 and E3 — parked openly, not faked).

The MVP-core sits where the primary persona's importance is highest and the market is thin: R2 (understand my pet fast), S1 (everyone on the same page), R3 (accurate picture for a vet) — the portable, role-based sharing whitespace. R1 is essential but table-stakes.

This matrix is the safety net: it proves nothing is decorative and nothing is missing.

Reading the columns catches orphan screens (a screen no job needs — likely scope creep); reading the rows catches orphan jobs (a job with nowhere to do it — a product gap). The 3 MVP-core jobs — R2 (understand my pet fast), S1 (keep everyone on the same page) and R3 (give a vet an accurate picture) — are the ones that are central to the primary persona and under-served by the market. R1 is deliberately treated as table-stakes, not a differentiator.

Two rows stay empty on purpose: R4 (proof my pet's okay while away) is parked in backlog — there's no carer→owner channel and no caregiver evidence yet; E3 (spare my pet stress) is out of scope — it's an offline outcome, not something a screen delivers.

06 — Rationale

Decisions & open questions

Why the architecture looks the way it does — the calls made, what was tempting but set aside, and the honest unknowns that should be validated before build.

Decisions we made

Three global tabs, not five

Each global item is the entry to a main-job verb: Pets (consult) · What's due (act in time) · Share (pass on). A nav slot has to earn its place with a job cluster.

Instead of: a tab per feature "because every app has one".

The Pet dossier is the spine

Everything hangs off one per-pet record — the "single trusted place" the main job is named for. Modules are views onto the dossier, not separate silos.

Instead of: feature-first menus (health here, docs there, no centre).

Single-pet auto-land

With one pet (the Organised Owner's typical case), the first screen is the dossier → main job in 0 taps. My Pets only matters with 2+ pets.

Instead of: always opening a pet-list the user must tap through.

1-tap emergency shortcut

Critical info is reachable in one tap from home — a worried moment (R5) can't afford the 3-tap dossier path.

Instead of: burying emergency info inside the dossier.

Recipients get a link, not an account

The caregiver opens a no-account, read-only link. Account/auth friction is a documented drop-off driver (research F7), and nobody signs up for a one-week sit.

Instead of: "create an account to view this pet".

In-app calendar + export to external calendars

What's due gets a built-in calendar view inside the app, plus an export option to Google Calendar, Apple Calendar, and Outlook so owners can see pet events alongside their personal schedule.

Updated: originally list-only with ICS parked — owner feedback confirmed both in-app and export are wanted.

Recovery, never dead ends

Every error/empty state offers a next step (retry / offline / re-share / show contact). Capture-friction is the adoption make-or-break (F4); stranding a user kills trust.

Instead of: happy-path-only flows.

A job behind every screen

The traceability matrix removed screens with no job (Archive/delete merged, Account-prefs cut) and parked jobs with no screen openly (R4, E3).

Instead of: settings/extra pages added out of habit.

Tempting, but set aside

  • Carer → owner "updates while away" (R4). Felt obvious — owners love Rover photo updates — but there's no validated need, no first-person caregiver voice, and no carer-update channel today. Parked, not faked.
  • In-app vet messaging / telehealth flavour. Confirmed: one-way record-sharing is enough for MVP. Vet messaging deferred.
  • Reviews / trust ratings / find-a-carer marketplace. Rover-shaped and natural-looking, but it maps only to a hypothesis job — PetPal does no matchmaking. Skipped entirely.
  • Breed-aware food database & product recommendations. In the brief, but addresses no user job and is maintenance-heavy. Cut to free-text owner notes.
  • An account / notification-settings screen. Reflex to add it, but no job behind it. Removed; the one real setting (reminder channel) folded into What's due.
  • "Spare my pet stress" as a feature (E3). A real owner motivation, but the stress reduction happens offline (the vet's room). No screen can serve it — out of scope, not invented.
  • White-label clinic app. A business-model ambition, not an owner/carer job. Deferred so it doesn't consume MVP design effort.

Questions — resolved

  • Do PetPal's own people actually want this? Resolved Yes. The owner uses PetPal as a personal handover pack for dog/cat sitters and as the single place for everything pet-related: behaviour notes, preferences, character, favourite snacks and games, periodic reminders (jabs, vet reviews, insurance), vet clinic details (address, doctor), insurance coverage and documents, passport and other pet documents. Future (post-MVP): vet clinics could use it as a GP-style dossier — move country, take the whole record with you.
  • Will a caregiver use the link, or fall back to "just text me"? Resolved The caregiver will use the link provided by the owner. It gives read-only access to the pet dossier, preferences, behaviour and health details, and emergency contacts. No messaging or video-call features for MVP.
  • Is the pain strong enough to switch from WhatsApp / Notes? Resolved Yes. The pet dossier is valuable for both owner and sitter. The emergency contacts section covers the urgent-info pain. WhatsApp is not abandoned — it remains an additional channel for calls, not the record system.
  • Should vet messaging be in the MVP at all? Resolved No. One-way record-sharing is enough for MVP. Vet messaging is deferred.
  • Will owners fill in emergency authorization? Resolved Not for MVP. Spend cap / payment / who-may-authorize adds too much capture friction with zero owner-side evidence. Cut from MVP scope.
  • Is calendar export wanted, and which calendar? Resolved Yes to both: an in-app calendar built into PetPal, plus export to Google Calendar, Apple Calendar, and Outlook.
  • What about non-dog/cat owners? Resolved MVP supports two pet types only: dogs and cats. Rabbit, guinea pig, and bird are deferred to a later phase.
  • Does single-pet auto-land confuse multi-pet owners? Resolved Not confusing as long as the UI clearly communicates which pet is active. Ideas: a tab or quick-switch button on the home screen to swap between pets; the same button could adapt per screen (home → pet switch, calendar → create event). The key: always signal which pet the user is interacting with.
